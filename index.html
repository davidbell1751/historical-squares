The First Four Squares

The first four squares were laid out by James Oglethorpe in 1733, the same year in which he founded the Georgia colony and the city of Savannah.
  • Johnson Square - Johnson Square was the first of Savannah's squares and remains the largest of the 24. It was named for Robert Johnson, colonial governor of South Carolina and a friend of General Oglethorpe.
  • Wright Square - The square is the burial site of Tomochichi, a leader of the Creek nation of Native Americans.
  • Ellis Square - It was also known as Marketplace Square, as from the 1730s through the 1950s it served as a center of commerce and was home to four successive market houses.
  • Telfair Square - It is the only square honoring a family rather than an individual.

1790's Squares

Savannah grew rapidly in the late 18th century and six new wards were established in the 1790s alone, including the four that now comprise the northeastern quadrant of the Historic District.
  • Washington Square - Built in 1790, Washington Square was named in 1791 for the first President of the United States, who visited Savannah in that year.
  • Franklin Square - It was named in 1791 for Benjamin Franklin who served as an agent for the colony of Georgia from 1768 to 1778 and who had died in 1790
  • Warren Square - Warren Square was laid out in 1791 and named for General Joseph Warren, a Revolutionary War hero killed at the Battle of Bunker Hill and who had served as President of the Provincial Government of Massachusetts.
  • Columbia Square - Columbia Square was laid out in 1799 and is named for Columbia, the poetic personification of the United States.
  • Greene Square - Greene Square was laid out in 1799 and is named for Revolutionary War hero General Nathanael Greene, an aide to George Washington.
  • Liberty Square - Liberty Square was laid out in 1799 and named in honor of the Sons of Liberty and the victory over the British in the Revolutionary War.

19th-Century Squares

  • Elbert Square - Elbert Square was laid out in 1801 and named for Samuel Elbert, a Revolutionary soldier, sheriff of Chatham County, and Governor of Georgia.
  • Chippewa Square - Chippewa Square was laid out in 1815 and named in honor of American soldiers killed in the Battle of Chippawa during the War of 1812
  • Orleans Square - Orleans Square was laid out in 1815, commemorating General Andrew Jackson's victory at the Battle of New Orleans in January of that year.
  • Lafayette Square - The square contains a fountain commemorating the 250th anniversary of the founding of the Georgia colony.
  • Pulaski Square - It is one of the few squares without a monument—General Pulaski's statue is actually in nearby Monterey Square.
  • Madison Square - Madison Square features vintage cannon from the Savannah Armory.
  • Crawford Square - During the era of Jim Crow this was the only square in which African-Americans were permitted.
  • Chatham Square - Chatham Square is sometimes known locally as Barnard Square, in reference to a city school that stood adjacent for many years.
  • Monterey Square - The square also is home to Congregation Mickve Israel, which boasts one of the few Gothic-style synagogues in America, dating from 1878.
  • Troup Square - A large iron armillary sphere stands in the center of the square, supported by six small metal turtles.
  • Calhoun Square - Calhoun Square was laid out in 1851 and is named for South Carolina statesman John C. Calhoun, who served as Secretary of War, Secretary of State, and as Vice President under John Quincy Adams and Andrew Jackson.
  • Whitefield Square - Whitefield Square was laid out in 1851—the final square built.
